LC-PolScope · polarized-light microscopy

How polarization states read out birefringence

Why it matters: an ordinary polarizing microscope shows that something is birefringent, but you can't read off how much retardance it has or which way its slow axis points. The LC-PolScope solves this by illuminating the sample with circularly polarized light and a liquid-crystal compensator that cycles the polarization state. Circular light alone reveals the retardance magnitude but is blind to orientation; adding the compensator's “swing” at different orientations makes the camera intensity modulate — its amplitude gives the retardance and its phase gives the slow-axis angle. Set a specimen, then sweep the compensator.

Circular illumination plus a compensator bias at orientation alpha passes a birefringent specimen and a circular analyzer; the intensity vs alpha is a sinusoid whose amplitude encodes retardance and phase encodes the slow-axis azimuth.
