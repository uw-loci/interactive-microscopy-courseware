Cleared-tissue light-sheet microscope

1Point-scanning vs. light sheet — photobleaching & speed

Why a light sheet at all? Speed and gentleness. A confocal focuses the laser down through the full depth at every point — bleaching the whole beam line and crawling plane‑by‑plane — while a light sheet lights one plane from the side and sweeps through fast, bleaching only what it images. That low photodamage and speed is what lets it image big cleared or living 3‑D samples; the trade-off is that the sample must be reachable from two perpendicular sides (illumination + detection). In side view the confocal beam bleaches the full-depth column at every scanned point and covers the volume slowly; the light sheet lights one plane at a time, sweeping quickly and bleaching only that plane.

2Cylindrical lens, objective & the NA tradeoff

Why the waist matters: it sets how thin — and how good — your optical sections are. The cylindrical lens shapes the round beam into a sheet (its height), upstream of two relay mirrors and two tube lenses; the illumination objective then focuses it to a thin waist, and the objective's NA trades that thickness against how far the sheet stays thin. 3Sheet thickness & scanning — what makes good data

Why thickness matters: it sets your image quality directly. The light sheet stays fixed and the sample is translated through it, one plane at a time. A thin sheet lights only the plane at the sheet — crisp sections; a thick one also lights the planes above and below, which blur in as haze. Drag the slider and watch the camera image.

A thin light sheet produces crisp images; a thick sheet adds out-of-focus haze and lowers contrast.

Optical section: crisp   contrast high.

4The galvo & the knife-edge mirror

Why two arms: lighting from both sides keeps the image even across the field. Tilting the galvo walks the beam across the knife‑edge mirror — left feeds arm 1, right feeds arm 2, dead centre splits to both — and the same galvo's fast dither pivots the sheet to kill shadows (widget 5). Switch arms, or auto‑sweep to watch the hand‑off.

Tilting the galvo routes the beam across a knife-edge mirror into arm 1 or arm 2; at the center it splits into both arms.

Routing: arm 1  

5Scanning the sheet to wipe out shadows

Why scan the sheet: dense spots cast shadows that wreck the image. An opaque speck blocks the sheet and casts a dark stripe downstream — a striping artifact. If the galvo dithers (pivots) the sheet through a small angle during the exposure, each speck is lit from many directions and the stripe averages away. Same sample, both ways:

Without scanning, opaque specks cast shadow stripes; pivoting the sheet during the exposure fills the shadows and removes the stripes.

6Axially swept light sheet (ASLM) — sweeping the waist with a voice coil

How this microscope stays razor‑thin across the whole field. A thin waist gives the sharpest optical sections — but a thin sheet is also short, staying thin over only a small stretch, so it can't cover the field on its own (widget 2). ASLM solves this: a voice coil in a remote‑focusing arm rocks a mirror back and forth, sliding the thin waist across the field of view, while the camera's rolling shutter opens only a narrow band that rides along with the waist — so only the thinnest, in‑focus slice is ever recorded. Match the sheet waist to the shutter and the section sharpens — but it also dims, because a thinner band collects less light. Drag both sliders.

A voice-coil mirror sweeps the light-sheet waist across the field of view while the camera rolling shutter tracks the waist, recording only the thin in-focus band. Narrowing the waist and shutter together sharpens the image but reduces its brightness; a shutter wider than the waist lets out-of-focus haze leak in.
